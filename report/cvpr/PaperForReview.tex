\begin{abstract}
Accurate plant disease recognition from leaf images is important for precision agriculture and early crop intervention. In this project, we study image-based plant disease classification on the PlantVillage dataset, which contains 54,305 images across 38 classes and 14 plant species. We compare transfer learning strategies and model families, including ResNet-50 trained from scratch, EfficientNet-B3 with linear probing and full fine-tuning, and ViT-Small with full fine-tuning. We also analyze the effect of input modality using color, grayscale, and background-segmented images. Our results show that pretrained models adapted end-to-end yield the strongest overall performance: EfficientNet-B3 achieves the best validation Macro-F1 of 99.54\% and the best test Macro-F1 of 99.75\% on color images. A ViT-Small pipeline is then used for modality ablation and GradCAM analysis, which further show that color is a critical diagnostic cue, whereas background removal provides only marginal benefit. Finally, GradCAM and GradCAM++ visualizations indicate that the model generally attends to lesion-bearing regions, with remaining errors concentrated in visually similar disease classes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Plant diseases significantly reduce agricultural productivity and can spread rapidly if not detected early. Manual diagnosis by domain experts is often costly and difficult to scale, especially when monitoring large crop areas. Computer vision offers a practical alternative by enabling automatic recognition of disease symptoms directly from leaf images.

Deep learning has substantially improved image classification performance, but plant disease recognition remains challenging in fine-grained multi-class settings. Different diseases may share similar textures, discoloration patterns, or lesion shapes, particularly within the same crop family. In addition, it is not always clear whether classification performance depends mainly on leaf symptoms, global color statistics, or background artifacts.

In this project, we investigate three questions: (1) how much transfer learning matters for PlantVillage classification, (2) whether removing the background improves performance, and (3) whether model predictions can be interpreted through saliency-based explainability. To answer these questions, we build a unified training pipeline, compare multiple architectures and training strategies, and analyze learned attention using GradCAM and GradCAM++. This framing is useful not only for achieving strong benchmark performance, but also for understanding what kinds of visual evidence modern models rely on in a relatively clean agricultural dataset. As a result, the project serves both as a classification study and as an analysis of representation quality, modality dependence, and trustworthiness of model behavior.

\section{Related Work}

PlantVillage has become a standard benchmark for plant disease classification since its introduction by Hughes and Salath\'e~\cite{hughes2015open}. Early work on this benchmark relied on convolutional neural networks with transfer learning from ImageNet, leveraging pretrained representations for improved data efficiency and feature quality. More recently, transformer-based architectures~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} and efficient scaling strategies~\cite{tan2019efficientnet,he2016resnet} have further advanced image recognition by capturing longer-range dependencies and richer global representations.

Beyond classification accuracy, explainability has become increasingly important in visual diagnosis tasks: growers and researchers often want evidence that a model responds to lesion regions rather than spurious background cues or dataset-specific artifacts. Gradient-based localization methods such as GradCAM~\cite{selvaraju2017grad} and GradCAM++~\cite{chattopadhay2018gradcampp} provide a widely used mechanism for this purpose by highlighting image areas that most influence the predicted class.

\section{Approach}

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

We use the PlantVillage dataset, which contains 54,305 leaf images from 38 disease/health classes across 14 plant species. Three input versions are considered: \textit{color}, \textit{grayscale}, and \textit{background-segmented}. All images are resized to $224 \times 224$. We use a fixed 80/10/10 split on the original dataset, resulting in 43,444 training, 5,430 validation, and 5,431 test images for the color version. The grayscale version reuses the same image identities. The background-segmented version follows the same source split when segmented counterparts are available; in the aligned evaluation used later, 5,318 of the 5,431 test images have segmented matches.

To improve generalization, we apply data augmentation during training: random horizontal flip, random vertical flip, random resized crop, color jitter, and MixUp. All models are optimized with cross-entropy loss using AdamW (weight decay $1{\times}10^{-4}$). The baseline uses a learning rate of $1{\times}10^{-3}$; all other experiments use $3{\times}10^{-4}$. Model selection is based on validation performance, and the best checkpoint for each experiment is used for final evaluation. This unified protocol ensures that differences across runs mainly reflect model design and transfer-learning strategy rather than inconsistent split construction or evaluation settings.

All experiments were implemented in PyTorch. Batch size and total training epochs were chosen according to the memory and convergence characteristics of each architecture, while keeping the data split and evaluation metrics fixed across settings.

\subsection{Model Comparison}

We compare four model-strategy settings on the color dataset:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ResNet-50 from scratch}~\cite{he2016resnet}: no pretrained weights;
    \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B3 linear probing}: pretrained backbone frozen, classifier head trained;
    \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B3 full fine-tuning}: pretrained model with all layers updated;
    \item \textbf{ViT-Small full fine-tuning}: pretrained transformer with all layers updated.
\end{itemize}

This setup isolates the effects of transfer learning and architecture choice. We report validation and test metrics using Top-1 accuracy and Macro-F1.

\subsection{Input-Modality Ablation}

After model comparison, we analyze the effect of input modality. We evaluate the selected strong pretrained transformer pipeline on the three dataset versions: color, grayscale, and background-segmented. This allows us to measure the contribution of color information and test whether removing the background simplifies the task.

\subsection{Explainability}

To interpret model behavior, we apply GradCAM and GradCAM++ to representative correct and incorrect predictions. We select representative correct examples across the 38 classes and representative incorrect examples from the classes in which test-set errors occur. The goal is to verify whether the model focuses on lesion-bearing regions and to diagnose failure modes in confusing classes.

\subsection{Baseline}

We first establish a baseline using a ResNet-18 model~\cite{he2016resnet} trained on the color dataset. This baseline achieves 97.53\% test accuracy and 96.34\% test Macro-F1, showing that a standard convolutional baseline is already highly effective on PlantVillage.

\subsection{Model Comparison}

\Cref{tab:model-selection} summarizes validation-set model selection on the color dataset. End-to-end adaptation of pretrained models is strongest: EfficientNet-B3 with full fine-tuning achieves the best validation Macro-F1 (99.54\%), with ViT-Small close behind at 98.89\%. The from-scratch ResNet-50 reaches 97.30\% validation Macro-F1, competitive but below the best pretrained configurations. Linear probing remains far weaker (58.01\% Macro-F1), indicating that under our current training setup, frozen pretrained features are markedly less effective than full fine-tuning for this fine-grained disease classification task. MixUp is applied uniformly across settings for consistency, although this may be less compatible with the frozen-backbone linear-probing setup, so the result should be interpreted with some caution.

\Cref{tab:test-results} reports held-out test metrics. The ResNet-18 baseline already performs strongly, EfficientNet-B3 with full fine-tuning is the top overall model at 99.83\% test accuracy and 99.75\% test Macro-F1, and ViT-Small remains a strong second pretrained model. For the subsequent ablation and explainability analysis, we use the ViT-Small pipeline to keep the modality comparison and GradCAM workflow on a single explainability-ready setup.

\subsection{Input-Modality Ablation}

\Cref{tab:ablation} shows the effect of input modality using the ViT-Small evaluation pipeline. Color images produce the best results. Converting the images to grayscale causes the largest drop, reducing test accuracy to 95.86\% and Macro-F1 to 94.46\%. This suggests that chromatic information is a major diagnostic cue for PlantVillage diseases. Background segmentation, by contrast, changes the result only slightly: on the aligned subset with 5,318 matched test images, accuracy is 99.25\% and Macro-F1 is 99.09\%. Because this background-segmented score is computed on a slightly smaller matched subset than the color and grayscale rows, it should be interpreted as a near-matched comparison rather than a perfectly identical test-set evaluation.

Even with the matched-subset caveat, the small gap between the color and background-segmented settings suggests that the benchmark is largely leaf-centric and that the classifier does not depend heavily on background context. By contrast, the substantial drop under grayscale conversion indicates that many disease categories are characterized not only by shape and texture but also by subtle color cues such as yellowing, browning, or rust-like pigmentation.


\subsection{Explainability and Failure Analysis}

As shown in \Cref{fig:gradcam-results}, GradCAM and GradCAM++ visualizations indicate that the model generally attends to lesion-bearing regions for correct predictions rather than irrelevant image areas. In the correctly classified tomato example, the highlighted regions broadly align with visible disease patterns, supporting the plausibility of the learned decision process.

\Cref{tab:hardest-classes} lists the hardest class for each input modality. The \textit{Potato / Healthy} class is consistently the most difficult, likely due to low test-set support (14 samples) and visual similarity to \textit{Soybean / Healthy}. The most frequent confusion pairs are: \textit{Peach / Bacterial Spot} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Tomato / Septoria Leaf Spot} (5 errors, color); \textit{Tomato / Spider Mites} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Tomato / Healthy} (21 errors, grayscale); and \textit{Tomato / Early Blight} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Tomato / Bacterial Spot} (7 errors, background-segmented).

For incorrect predictions, the attention becomes less discriminative and more diffuse, often spreading across larger leaf regions without isolating the most diagnostic symptoms. This behavior is consistent with our error analysis, where visually similar disease classes remain the most challenging. In grayscale, the failure modes become more severe, especially for tomato diseases such as \textit{Early blight}, \textit{Target Spot}, and \textit{Spider mites}, further confirming that color is an important discriminative cue. An additional observation from \Cref{fig:gradcam-results} is that the segmented inputs produce attention maps that are visually similar to those of the color inputs, which aligns with the quantitative ablation where background removal yields only marginal changes. Therefore, the primary remaining challenge is class ambiguity among diseases with similar visual symptoms rather than background clutter. From a practical perspective, this also suggests that future improvements are more likely to come from better fine-grained representation learning or harder example mining than from further preprocessing of the already-clean PlantVillage backgrounds. Another implication is that explainability can help distinguish between meaningful confidence and accidental shortcut behavior. When the heatmaps align with lesion structure, the prediction is easier to trust; when the response is broad or unstable, the model output should be treated more cautiously. This type of qualitative inspection is especially valuable in agricultural applications, where deployment decisions may depend not only on top-line accuracy but also on whether the model behaves consistently across disease presentations.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a systematic study of plant disease recognition on PlantVillage using transfer learning, modality ablation, and explainability analysis. Our experiments show that full fine-tuning of pretrained models provides the best overall results, with EfficientNet-B3 achieving the top validation and test Macro-F1 on the color dataset. The ViT-Small pipeline remains strong and serves as the basis for the modality ablation and GradCAM analysis. The weak linear-probing result should be interpreted as a result under the current training configuration, since MixUp may be less compatible with the frozen-backbone setting. The ablation demonstrated that color is a critical signal, whereas background removal provides only marginal benefit. Finally, GradCAM-based analysis showed that the model usually focuses on lesion-relevant regions, with errors concentrated in visually similar disease categories. Taken together, these findings suggest that PlantVillage is best viewed not simply as an easy benchmark but as a useful testbed for understanding how transfer learning, visual modality, and interpretability interact in fine-grained agricultural recognition. A limitation of this study is that the standard random image-level split on a highly curated benchmark may yield somewhat optimistic estimates, and the clean PlantVillage setting does not fully reflect real field conditions. Future work could therefore explore class-balanced optimization and evaluation on less curated real-world datasets. More broadly, our results highlight that even when overall accuracy is already very high, careful comparison of training strategy and input representation still reveals meaningful differences in model behavior. This makes the benchmark a useful educational setting for studying both performance and reliability in computer vision systems.
